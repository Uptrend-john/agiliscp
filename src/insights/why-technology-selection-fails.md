Choosing the right enterprise technology platform — whether it's an ERP, HCM, CRM, or any other critical system — is one of the most consequential decisions an organization can make. The right choice accelerates growth and efficiency. The wrong choice creates years of frustration, rework, and lost opportunity.

Yet many organizations approach technology selection in ways that virtually guarantee suboptimal outcomes.

## The Three Most Common Mistakes

### 1. Starting with the Technology, Not the Business

The most pervasive mistake is beginning the selection process by evaluating vendors before clearly defining what the business actually needs. When you lead with vendor demos and feature comparisons, you're letting the technology drive the conversation instead of your business requirements.

**The fix**: Start with a thorough requirements gathering process that's rooted in your business strategy, process needs, and future-state vision. Only then should you begin evaluating which solutions can best support those needs.

### 2. Letting Vendor Relationships Drive Decisions

Every major technology vendor has skilled sales teams and compelling demonstrations. But a great demo doesn't guarantee a great fit. Too often, organizations choose a platform because of an existing relationship, a compelling presentation, or industry buzz — rather than objective evaluation against their specific requirements.

**The fix**: Implement a structured, vendor-neutral scoring process. Weight your evaluation criteria based on business impact. Require vendors to demonstrate against *your* scenarios, not their pre-built scripts.

### 3. Underestimating Total Cost of Ownership

License costs are just the beginning. Implementation services, data migration, integrations, training, change management, ongoing support, and future upgrades often cost 3-5x the initial license investment. Organizations that don't account for TCO end up with budget surprises that compromise the entire initiative.

**The fix**: Build a comprehensive 5-year TCO model that includes all direct and indirect costs. Compare vendors on TCO, not just license price. Factor in internal resource requirements and opportunity costs.

## A Better Approach

Successful technology selection follows a disciplined process:

1. **Define the business case** — What outcomes are you trying to achieve?
2. **Map requirements** — What must the solution do vs. nice-to-have?
3. **Assess the market** — Which vendors can credibly meet your needs?
4. **Structure the evaluation** — Demos, references, proof of concept
5. **Analyze objectively** — Weighted scoring, TCO comparison, risk assessment
6. **Negotiate strategically** — Leverage competition and protect your interests

## The Value of an Independent Advisor

Having an independent advisor in your corner — someone who knows the vendor landscape but isn't beholden to any vendor — can dramatically improve your selection outcomes. They bring pattern recognition from dozens of similar evaluations, negotiation leverage, and the objectivity to push back when something doesn't add up.
